Marginally Interesting…

2014-08-31cs scheme

SICP   

Sometimes I get the feeling that I merely rehash concepts or opinions that I seem to hear or read everywhere, all the time. But then again, many people I talk to have obviously never heard of these things. So probably it’s just that the selection of things I read online, or think about, or consider important, is heavily biased (even inside a particular domain). And quite likely, most people out there have their own biased selection. Here is a reminder of something I cherish, garnished with a few links — I would be sincerely happy if this piece would help only a single reader to find something useful outside of his/her usual track.

Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs is a classic MIT computer science textbook by Abelson and Sussman. I am not so much impressed by the remarkable (but nowhere near sufficient) impact it had on CS curricula all over the world. I am just baffled, time and again, by the fact that it still contains some of the most profound things I have read on computer science.1

The text is written in a beautifully lucid style. There are people who consider the writing obfuscated. My only explanation is that those people either did not expect an actual theoretical work, or they might think that a theoretical work has to delve into highly mathematical/technical CS areas. But keep in mind that this is an introductory text. If the “science” in “computer science” means anything, it is this: Abstracting from petty details, carving out the core ideas behind things and relating them to each other, getting closer to a systematic and conceptual understanding.

The range of topics is amazing, effortlessly ranging all the way from evaluation rules of simple procedures to writing compilers. The book shows that there are multiple genuinely different programming paradigms, and implements and explains them (and their relations).2 It discusses and really(!) explains computational models, closures, recursion, lambda expressions, symbolic data, concurrency, lazy evaluation and much more. Combined with the fact that the book came out in 1984 (and all those things had already been implemented at least a decade before that), it also shows that the new “features” that so-called industry-standard languages in 2014 take pride in (or still have not gotten right) are but a faint shadow of the really substantial thoughts and contributions in the field (glossing over the 40-year delay).

As a side note, I believe that a good part of the quality of the book is owed to its choice of programming language, Scheme. This does not detract the slightest bit from the authors’ marvelous presentation. Quite the opposite, they happen to be key figures in Scheme’s development themselves. This is not a coincidence: They wanted a language to clearly examine and demonstrate the essence of software and computing.

What does it mean that they went on to write a CS text using that language, and that this book appears as if it could hardly have been written in any other language? It means that Scheme is the natural language to cleanly and precisely formulate all the ideas and concepts we have about computing, in the most beautiful way possible. How could you not love such a language?

Why do I write this today? Because far too late, I found this nice current-tech rendition of the online text.

Do yourself a favor and have a look some time. If you have a soft spot for science and for quality and doing things right (as opposed to just getting stuff done somehow), you will surely appreciate it. It seems many people feel this way, here’s one apt review, and another one by no less than Peter Norvig.3

Myself, I started with the videos of the charmingly old-school original blackboard lectures, and frankly, I adore them. They gave me a vivid ideal of what the use of computers could look like in a more… civilized age.

And I have not yet abandoned all hope.
