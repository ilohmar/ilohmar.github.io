In 80 Characters Around the World   

Some debates rear their ugly heads now and then, and I manage to avoid them for some time, like, you know, grown-up style. But at some point somebody might just press all the right buttons at the right time, and instead of him, you have to suffer the following rant if you so choose…

The Issue

I am one of those people who think that monospaced on-screen text should stick to a line length limit of 80 characters. “80” is not some magic number, just a traditional one for old-school terminal machines AFAIK. Some might say 72 (to have some spare room), others say 78, it does not matter to the topic at hand. I was originally focusing on editing source code, but will also share a few thoughts on other content below.

The Trigger: a Straw Man Argument

We have large high-resolution monitors now, so why limit ourselves to 80 characters per line like in the seventies?

Being the browsing monkey that I am, it’s sadly not the first time I heard/read something like that.

Of course, nobody said that we should use an 80-char limit because of lack of space on the screen. While it might appear that this was the reason for the limit 35 years ago (I doubt it, but have not cared enough to investigate), that does not say anything about what’s right today.

The argument for an 80-characters limit is about readability and text recognition. It is much harder to get a good overview from a quick glance at wider lines, and it is much harder to follow text and line breaks closely on wider lines.

Bigger screens with high resolution are a wonderful thing — I do not want to work on anything less than a 24“ 1920x1200 monitor these days (better yet, two of them). However, if the best use of that width that you can think of is to use lines of 120 characters, or maybe 160 characters (yes, indeed), or as many as you see fit, you might want to work on your imagination.

Some suggestions! Maybe you like to keep a larger distance between you and the screen, and you like text at >= 24pt. That is an honorable thing to do. Or you want to see more content on-screen — my personal use is to have several programs open side-by-side (use a tiling window manager), or several files within one program.1

But having a single file, and just letting lines extend to the right is both, the worst possible use of your large screen (since, if there’s still hope, many lines will not be as wide), and a really bad choice for text comprehension and/or cursory overview.

On Prose

Writing prose is subject to the same thoughts (even more strongly in some sense, since there is much less typographical expression of syntax in most human languages compared to most programming languages, to my knowledge). Interestingly, however, the misconception that long lines “better exploit screen space” has instead led some authors of technical papers or research articles to add more weirdness on top. To always make “best” use of the horizontal space you happen to have available right now, you “should” use so-called “soft” line-wrapping, where your application breaks lines according to the width of your visual editing window (instead of you manually breaking the lines, or the program doing it at some fixed character count or visual width).

But that is turning things upside-down. Using your screen space well, as I tried to convince you, is about anything but making text as wide as possible. Therefore, I am a firm advocate of using hard line-breaks. This should not be confused with the output that a word-processor might produce, and which should, obviously, not adhere to such line breaks.

And at that point, one more terrible concept enters the stage: WYSIWYG for word processors, almost eliminating the distinction between form and content, and just begging for the misconceptions and fallacies described above. But actually, this might be for another post…

One Last Thing

Even in the best of programming languages, there are times when long lines seem unavoidable, or the least of several evils. So be it! But if you find yourself in this dilemma often, this could say something about the tools you use (such as, mainly, the programming language), or about the style of the code, or both. Whatever the problem is, don’t try to push it beyond the 80-characters fence.
